Parkinson's disease

Parkinson’s disease is neurodegenerative brain disorder that causes unintended or uncontrollable movements, such as shaking, stiffness and difficulty with balance and coordination.

While virtually anyone could be at risk of developing Parkinson’s, it usually affects more men than women. Another distinct risk is age. It is highly unlikely for a healthy person under the age of 20 to be diagnosed with this illness.

Just like Alzheimer’s disease, symptoms will gradually worsen over time. The most prominent signs and symptoms occur when nerve cells in the basal ganglia (an area of the brain that controls movement) becomes impaired and/or dies. Usually, this part of the brain produces dopamine and without it, the afflicted person suffers from movement problems. The 4 main symptoms are tremors in the limbs or their stiffness, slowness in overall movement and imbalance in coordination. 

Early symptoms will usually be seen by closest family or friends, where the suffering one will show unusual changes in movement including issues with standing up, mild tremors, different speech patterns, lack of facial expressions or unusual handwriting.
